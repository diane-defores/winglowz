# [The art of priority. Action expresses priorities. | by Asbed Boymoushakian | Medium](https://medium.com/@asbedb/the-art-of-priority-a68999a424a7)

![](IV.%20Prioritization-202411230413.png)
**Action expresses priorities.**

A poignant quote that resonated with me very early in my career, after over a decade of trial end error, the creed found its place on my mantle of philosophy and rightfully drove the style I nurtured to be my eventual work ethic.

The ability to prioritize effectively can be the difference between thriving and merely surviving. Enter the Eisenhower Matrix — a powerful tool that can transform how you approach your work and boost both your productivity and sense of accomplishment. Below ill share how this simple yet effective method can revolutionize your workday.

Named after President Dwight D. Eisenhower, this prioritization system divides tasks into four quadrants based on their urgency and importance:

1. **Urgent and Important:***Do immediately*
2. **Important but Not Urgent:***Schedule*
3. **Urgent but Not Important:***Delegate*
4. **Neither Urgent nor Important:** *Eliminate*

By categorizing your tasks this way, you gain clarity on what truly deserves your attention and what actions will yield the most significant results.

**To apply the Eisenhower Matrix to your work:**

1. List all your tasks and projects.
2. Assess each item’s urgency and importance.
3. Place each task in its appropriate quadrant.
4. Act accordingly: do, schedule, delegate, or eliminate.

For example, a critical client presentation due tomorrow falls into “Urgent and Important,” while long-term strategic planning is “Important but Not Urgent.” Responding to non-critical emails might be “Urgent but Not Important,” and can often be delegated. Mindless social media scrolling? That’s likely “Neither Urgent nor Important” and should be eliminated or severely limited.

Adopting the Eisenhower Matrix can lead to remarkable efficiency improvements:

1. Reduced Time Waste: By identifying and eliminating low-value activities, you free up time for what truly matters.
2. Improved Focus: Concentrating on high-impact tasks ensures your energy is spent where it counts most.
3. Better Time Management: Scheduling important but non-urgent tasks prevents last-minute rushes and reduces stress.

1. Conduct a Task Audit: List everything you do in a typical week.
2. Categorize: Place each task in its appropriate quadrant.
3. Analyze: Look for patterns. Are you spending too much time on urgent but unimportant tasks?
4. Adjust: Realign your schedule to focus more on important tasks, both urgent and non-urgent.
5. Review Regularly: Make this process a weekly habit to stay on track.

The Eisenhower Matrix is more than just a time management tool; it’s a philosophy for professional excellence. By consistently prioritizing what’s truly important, you not only become more efficient but also more effective. You’ll find yourself accomplishing more meaningful work, feeling less overwhelmed, and experiencing a greater sense of achievement in your professional life.

Remember, the art of priority isn’t about doing more — it’s about doing what matters most.

## What is the RICE Scoring Model for Prioritization?

The RICE scoring model is a [prioritization framework](https://www.productplan.com/product-management-frameworks/) designed to help product managers determine which products, features, and other initiatives to put on their roadmaps by scoring these items according to four factors. These factors, which form the acronym RICE, are reach, impact, confidence, and effort.

Using a scoring model such as RICE can offer product teams a three-fold benefit. First, it can enable product managers to make better-informed decisions, minimize personal biases in decision making, and help them defend their priorities to other stakeholders such as the executive staff.
**RICE Scoring Model:**

- **Definition**: The RICE scoring model is a tool to help product managers decide which projects to prioritize. It stands for Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort.

**History**:
- **Origin**: Developed by Intercom to address their unique decision-making needs.
- **Purpose**: To create an objective way to prioritize different project ideas.

**How it Works**:
- **Reach**: Estimate how many people will be affected by the project within a specific timeframe. Example: If 150 new customers are expected in the next quarter, the reach score is 150.
- **Impact**: Assess how much the project will affect the audience. Intercom uses the following scale:
  - 3 = massive impact
  - 2 = high impact
  - 1 = medium impact
  - 0.5 = low impact
  - 0.25 = minimal impact
- **Confidence**: Measure how confident you are about your estimates. Intercom’s scale:
  - 100% = high confidence
  - 80% = medium confidence
  - 50% = low confidence
  - Below 50% is considered a "moonshot" and should not be prioritized.
- **Effort**: Calculate the resources needed, measured in person-months. For example, a project taking three person-months has an effort score of 3.

**Formula**: \( \text{RICE Score} = \frac{\text{Reach} \times \text{Impact} \times \text{Confidence}}{\text{Effort}} \)

**Conclusion**:
- Using the RICE model helps create a consistent and objective method for evaluating project value.
- It is especially useful if other prioritization methods have not worked.
